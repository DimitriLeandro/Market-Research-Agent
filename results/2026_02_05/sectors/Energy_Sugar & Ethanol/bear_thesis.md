### **1. Structural Risks: Threats to Long-Term Viability**

*   **Falling Sugar Prices:** International sugar prices have fallen approximately 28% over the past year, reaching around 14 cents a pound, the lowest level in five years. At this price, sugar production is barely covering costs, even for Brazil, the world's most competitive producer. *   **Ethanol Price Pressure:** The outlook for ethanol is also adding to the pressure. Falling oil prices are intensifying competition with gasoline, while a growing supply of corn-based ethanol is weighing on prices.
*   **Competition from Corn Ethanol:** A boom in corn ethanol production in Brazil is diverting sugarcane processors away from the biofuel market, leading them to produce more sugar, which is contributing to the slump in global sugar prices. Corn-based output is forecast to account for 32% of Brazil's fuel ethanol production, up from 23%.

*   **Oversupply:** The sugar and ethanol sector has suffered from oversupply, which has reduced the prices of these commodities in both domestic and foreign markets. Global production will exceed consumption by 2.8 million metric tons.

*   **High Interest Rates:** High interest rates in Brazil increase the financial costs for refineries. High interest rates impact the cost of raising funds used to finance agriculture. The interest rate in Brazil was around 14% per year between 2015 and 2016.
*   **Economic Shocks:** The Brazilian sugar and ethanol sector has been affected by a diversity of economic shocks within their own sector and outside, i.e. at government, national and global levels.
*   **Credit Availability:** Reduced and more expensive financing for sugar and ethanol companies by Brazilian banks.

### 3. Regulatory Threats

*   **Government Intervention:** Government control over gasoline prices can negatively impact the ethanol sector.
*   **Tax Changes:** Institutional risks, such as tax changes, can affect strategies and competition in the production chain.
*   **Doubtful Tax Incentives:** Some state tax incentives have a doubtful legal basis.

### 4. Competition

*   **Corn Ethanol:** Rising competition from cheaper corn-based biofuel is forcing many mills to remain focused on sugar, even if prices stay weak.
*   **Global Competition:** The Brazilian sugar and ethanol sector faces high competition.
*   **U.S. Tariffs:** The U.S. demand for Brazilian sugar could take a hit if tariffs go through. Brazil's exports were facing a 10% minimum rate but now they're staring down the possibility of 50% tariffs.

This Bear Case highlights several significant risks that could negatively impact the Brazilian Energy/Sugar & Ethanol sector.
